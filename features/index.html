How does CRISPR work?

CRISPR sequences are transcribed into short RNA sequences, known as crRNAs, which guides the system to target DNA. The enzyme Cas9 (produced by the CRISPR system) binds to the DNA and cuts it, while shutting the targeted gene down. Modified versions of Cas9 are able to activate the gene, rather than shutting it off, all of which allows researchers to study the gene’s function. Additionally, this allows researchers to edit the genomes of organisms.

Types of gene edits possible with CRISPR

Disruption

With the help of the guide RNA, CRISPR-Cas9 makes a cut on the DNA. This cut is repaired by the DNA’s natural repair mechanisms, resulting in the addition or deletion of base pair(s) and deactivating the gene.

Deletion

A portion of the DNA is cut out by two guide RNAs with separate targets. The two separate ends within the DNA are now joined together, and the portion of DNA is removed.

Insertion/Correction

By including another gene sequence to act as a template with the CRISPR-Cas9 system, it possible to replace/correct faulty genes with a functional gene, or insert a new gene into the DNA sequence.

Furthermore, gene edits made through CRISPR can be categorised by whether they occur interal or external to the organism being genetically modified.
