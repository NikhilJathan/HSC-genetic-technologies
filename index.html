What is CRISPR?

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Palindromic Repeats) is a gene editing technology, derived from a bacterial defence system. CRISPR and CRISPR-Cas9 are used generally to refer to the various CRISPR-Cas9, -CPF1 and other systems that target specific sections of genetic code to edit.

1993

  • Discovery of CRISPR locus

  • Francisco Mojica became the first researcher to characterise what is called a ‘CRISPR locus’; correctly hypothesising that it was an adaptive immune system.

2005

  • Discovery of PAM
  • Alexander Bolotin found an unusual CRISPR locus, lacking some of the already discovered Cas genes, and instead having new cas genes. This sequence, PAM that Bolotin discovered, is now used for target recognition in CRISPR technologies.

2008

  • Discovery that crRNAs guide Cas proteins
  • Luciano Marraffini and Erik Sontheimer demonstrated that crRNAs guide the Cas proteins to target DNA molecules. They noted that this system could be a powerful tool if it could be used in non-bacterial systems.

2010

  • CRISPR-Cas9 creates double-strand breaks
  • Sylvain Moineau and colleagues showed that CRISPR-Cas9 creates double-strand breaks in precise locations.

2011

  • Discovery of tracrRNA
  • Emmanuelle Charpentier and colleagues performed RNA sequencing on Streptococcus pyogenes, finding that another small RNA which they called tracrRNA, forms a duplex with crRNA that guides Cas9 to targets.

2013

  • CRISPR-Cas9 used for genome editing for the first time
  • Feng Zhang,who had experience working with other genome editing technologies such as TALENs became the first person to successfully adapt CRISPR-Cas9 for genome editing in eukaryotic cells.
  • Commercial use of CRISPR
  • CRISPR therapeutics was founded, a company aiming to further research in applications of CRISPR for gene therapy.

2023

  • Gene Therapy using CRISPR-Cas9
  • The first-ever CRISPR-based therapy is approved in the United States, Great Britain and Bahrain.
